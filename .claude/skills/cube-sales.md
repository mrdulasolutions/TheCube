### THE SIX FACES

**FACE 1 -- FRAME CONTROL (F) -- Oren Klaff**
Sales is won or lost based on who controls the frame. The buyer's "crocodile brain" filters out most pitches. You must create novelty, tension, and status alignment. The seller is the prize, not the pursuer.
*"He who owns the frame wins."*
Self-prompt: "Who holds the frame right now? What frames are competing? How do I set, hold, or break the frame?"

**FACE 2 -- SPIN SELLING (B) -- Neil Rackham**
Complex sales are won by asking the right questions, not by closing harder. Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-Payoff -- a research-proven sequence that guides the buyer to articulate their own need.
*"The buyer should be doing most of the talking."*
Self-prompt: "What questions am I NOT asking? What does the buyer need to discover for themselves?"

**FACE 3 -- RAPPORT & INFLUENCE (L) -- Dale Carnegie**
Influence flows from genuine interest in others. People buy from those they like and trust. Win someone over by making them feel important, listening deeply, and talking in terms of their interests.
*"You can make more friends in two months by becoming interested in other people than in two years by trying to get people interested in you."*
Self-prompt: "Am I genuinely interested in this person? Do they feel heard, valued, and important?"

**FACE 4 -- CHALLENGER SALE (R) -- Dixon & Adamson**
The best reps do not just build relationships -- they challenge the customer's thinking. Teach, Tailor, Take Control. Lead with a commercial insight that reframes how the buyer sees their problem.
*"The best salespeople don't just build relationships; they challenge them."*
Self-prompt: "What does this buyer believe that is wrong? What insight can I teach them that changes how they see their world?"

**FACE 5 -- NEED SATISFACTION (U) -- Zig Ziglar**
Selling is a transference of belief. If you genuinely believe your product helps people, closing is an act of service. Identify what the prospect wants, then show how your product fulfills that desire.
*"You can have everything in life you want, if you will just help enough other people get what they want."*
Self-prompt: "What does this person actually want? How does what I offer genuinely help them get it?"

**FACE 6 -- PAIN QUALIFICATION (D) -- David Sandler**
Traditional selling creates adversarial dynamics. Instead, qualify ruthlessly for pain. Use the pain funnel to uncover real problems, not surface complaints. Let unqualified prospects disqualify themselves.
*"Don't spill your candy in the lobby."*
Self-prompt: "What is their real pain? Have I qualified it deeply enough? Should this prospect even be in my pipeline?"

### OPPOSITE FACE PAIRS (Rotation Axes)

- **X-Axis (F<>B):** Frame Control x SPIN Selling -- **Control vs Discovery**. Klaff says you must own the frame or the buyer's instincts reject you — the seller is the prize, not the pursuer. Rackham's research shows complex sales are won by asking questions, not asserting — the buyer should do most of the talking. These are opposite leadership styles. The tension reveals when to lead the conversation and when to let the buyer lead themselves to the close.

- **Y-Axis (U<>D):** Need Satisfaction x Pain Qualification -- **Desire vs Pain**. Ziglar believes selling is a transfer of belief — if you genuinely help people get what they want, you earn the right to close. Sandler believes unqualified optimism wastes everyone's time — dig into real pain or walk away. One sells toward pleasure, the other sells away from fear. The tension reveals which motivational direction actually unlocks this specific buyer.

- **Z-Axis (L<>R):** Rapport x Challenger -- **Warmth vs Hard Truth**. Carnegie built an empire on genuine warmth: become genuinely interested in people, make them feel important, never criticize. Dixon's research found that Challengers — reps who teach, tailor, and create constructive tension — consistently outperform Relationship Builders. The tension reveals whether this buyer needs to feel comfortable or needs to be pushed out of their current thinking.

## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

Follow the same UX Protocol as the core Cube: opening banner (with "Sales Stack" subtitle), progress indicators, Key Insight tags after every position, phase transition markers, cross-references, and Complexity Assessment after Face Scan.

### STEP 0: PROBLEM INTAKE

If the user provided a sales problem with the command, use it. Otherwise prompt:

> **What sales challenge would you like to put inside The Cube?**
>
> Describe the deal, prospect, objection, pipeline issue, or sales strategy question. The more specific -- deal size, buyer type, stage, blockers -- the sharper the analysis.

### STEP 1: CORE LOCK

```
## THE CORE

**Situation:** [1-2 sentence crystallized sales challenge]
**Stakes:** [What is at risk -- revenue, relationship, pipeline, market position]
**Current Approach:** [How you are currently selling this -- the frame The Cube will break out of]
```

### STEP 2: FACE SCAN (Positions 1-6)

Apply each methodology independently to the sales situation. 4-8 sentences each, ending with Key Insight.

**[F] Position 1: Frame Control (Klaff)** -- Who holds the frame? What frames compete? How to set/hold/break?
**[B] Position 2: SPIN Selling (Rackham)** -- Map the SPIN sequence: Situation → Problem → Implication → Need-Payoff. What questions should you ask?
**[L] Position 3: Rapport & Influence (Carnegie)** -- How strong is the relationship? What does the buyer care about? Are you genuinely interested?
**[R] Position 4: Challenger Sale (Dixon/Adamson)** -- What commercial insight can you teach? What does the buyer believe that is wrong? Where can you take control?
**[U] Position 5: Need Satisfaction (Ziglar)** -- What does the buyer actually want? How does your offer fulfill that desire? Is your belief in the product strong enough?
**[D] Position 6: Pain Qualification (Sandler)** -- What is the real pain? How deep does it go (pain funnel)? Is this prospect even qualified?

Display Complexity Assessment after all 6 faces.

### STEP 3: EDGE ROTATIONS (Positions 7-18)

3-5 sentences each. Focus on the emergent cross-insight. End with Key Insight.

#### [F-U] Position 7: "The Pitch" (Frame Control + Need Satisfaction)
*Klaff's frame dominance meets Ziglar's genuine service — you control the room AND serve the buyer's real desires.*
**Prompt:** "How do you control the frame while genuinely meeting the buyer's needs? Where does frame control enhance the sale, and where does it undermine trust?"

#### [F-R] Position 8: "The Power Play" (Frame Control + Challenger)
*Klaff's 'I am the prize' posture meets Dixon's 'teach them something new' mandate — maximum assertiveness from two directions.*
**Prompt:** "Frame control + commercial teaching = the most assertive possible approach. When is this exactly right? When does it push too hard?"

#### [F-D] Position 9: "The Trap Door" (Frame Control + Pain Qualification)
*You hold Klaff's frame while Sandler's pain funnel pulls the buyer deeper into their own problems — controlled descent.*
**Prompt:** "You hold the frame. They discover their pain. How do you control the conversation's direction while letting the buyer go deep into their own problems?"

#### [F-L] Position 10: "The Velvet Hammer" (Frame Control + Rapport)
*Klaff says own the frame or lose the deal. Carnegie says make them feel genuinely important. These seem contradictory — but the best closers do both.*
**Prompt:** "Klaff says own the frame. Carnegie says make them feel important. These seem contradictory. Where is the synthesis? How do you lead without alienating?"

#### [B-U] Position 11: "The Discovery Close" (SPIN + Need Satisfaction)
*Rackham's structured questions surface the need. Ziglar's philosophy says once you know the need, closing is an act of service — the buyer closes themselves.*
**Prompt:** "SPIN questions surface the need. Ziglar says fulfill it. How does the questioning sequence naturally lead to the buyer closing themselves?"

#### [B-R] Position 12: "The Teaching Question" (SPIN + Challenger)
*Rackham asks questions to discover. Dixon asks questions to teach and reframe. Together — questions that don't just reveal the problem, they change how the buyer sees it.*
**Prompt:** "Instead of just discovering -- teach through questions. What Implication and Need-Payoff questions would reframe how the buyer sees their problem?"

#### [B-D] Position 13: "The Pain Funnel" (SPIN + Pain Qualification)
*Rackham's Situation→Problem→Implication→Need-Payoff sequence meets Sandler's rule: no real pain, no real deal. Together — the deepest diagnostic in sales.*
**Prompt:** "SPIN's structured questioning meets Sandler's pain funnel. What questions go beyond surface problems to uncover the real, emotional, budget-impacting pain?"

#### [B-L] Position 14: "The Trusted Advisor" (SPIN + Rapport)
*Rackham's discovery questions powered by Carnegie's genuine curiosity — the buyer opens up because the interest is real, not rehearsed.*
**Prompt:** "How does genuine curiosity (Carnegie) make SPIN questions more effective? How does the buyer's experience change when questions come from real interest vs technique?"

#### [U-L] Position 15: "The Win-Win" (Need Satisfaction + Rapport)
*Ziglar's 'help enough people get what they want' meets Carnegie's authentic interest in people — the warmest, most relationship-driven approach in the cube.*
**Prompt:** "Ziglar's service ethic + Carnegie's genuine interest = the most relationship-driven approach. When is this the right play? What kind of buyer responds to this?"

#### [U-R] Position 16: "The Breakthrough" (Need Satisfaction + Challenger)
*Ziglar says fulfill the desire. Dixon says the buyer doesn't know what they need yet — teach them first. Together — you satisfy a need the buyer hasn't recognized.*
**Prompt:** "What if the buyer doesn't know what they need? Challenger teaches, Ziglar serves. How do you satisfy a need the buyer hasn't recognized yet?"

#### [D-L] Position 17: "The Empathetic Qualifier" (Pain Qualification + Rapport)
*Sandler's pain funnel can feel surgical. Carnegie's warmth creates safety. Together — the buyer reveals real pain because they feel genuinely cared for, not interrogated.*
**Prompt:** "Sandler's pain funnel can feel aggressive. Carnegie's warmth can feel soft. Together -- empathetic qualification. How do you create enough safety for the buyer to reveal real pain?"

#### [D-R] Position 18: "The Hard Truth" (Pain Qualification + Challenger)
*Sandler has qualified real pain. Dixon has a commercial insight that challenges their current thinking. Together — maximum honesty delivered with surgical precision.*
**Prompt:** "You know their pain is real. You have an insight that challenges their current approach. How do you deliver hard truth about their pain while maintaining the relationship?"

### STEP 4: AXIS TENSIONS (Positions 19-21)

4-6 sentences each exploring the dialectical tension.

#### [X-Axis] Position 19: "The Negotiation Table" (Frame Control x SPIN)
*Control vs Discovery. Who leads?*
**Prompt:** "Klaff says control the conversation. Rackham says let the buyer discover through questions. These are opposite leadership styles. For THIS sales situation, which yields better results? Is there a sequence -- control first, then discover? Or discover first, then take control?"

#### [Y-Axis] Position 20: "The Motivation Spectrum" (Need Satisfaction x Pain Qualification)

#### [Z-Axis] Position 21: "The Relationship Paradox" (Rapport x Challenger)
*Warmth vs Hard Truth. Be liked or be respected?*
**Prompt:** "Carnegie says never criticize. Challenger says the best reps create constructive tension. The Challenger research explicitly found that Challengers outperform Relationship Builders. But Carnegie's principles have built empires. For THIS buyer -- warmth or challenge? Can you do both?"

### STEP 6: RETURN TO HOME -- Grand Synthesis [Position 30]

Follow the core Cube synthesis protocol:

**6a. Executive Summary** -- 4 lines: the real sales challenge, clearest approach, biggest risk, key trade-off.
**6b. Pattern Detection** -- Themes across rotations.
**6c. Convergence Map** -- Where multiple positions agree on the same approach.
**6d. Divergence Report** -- Which selling styles contradict each other for this deal.
**6e. Blind Spot Report** -- What only one rotation revealed.
**6f. Deal Strategy** -- Rank top 3 approaches with:
- Recommended methodology mix (which faces to lead with)
- Buyer profile match (what type of buyer this approach suits)
- Risk of this approach backfiring
- Key phrases or moves to execute it
**6g. Simulation Scenarios** -- Role-play setups for each approach (opening line, anticipated objection, response strategy).
**6h. Final Verdict** -- The single best approach for THIS specific deal and why.

## EXECUTION NOTES

1. **Specificity over generality.** "Build rapport" is not an insight. "Ask about the VP's recent operations restructuring that you saw in their Q3 earnings call" is an insight.
2. **Honor the methodologies.** Each face represents a real, researched methodology. Apply it as its creator intended, then let the cross-analysis find the synthesis.
3. **The deal is the Core.** Never lose sight of the specific deal, buyer, and stage. Every rotation should move closer to a specific, executable sales action.
4. **Simulation scenarios should be role-playable.** Include specific opening lines, anticipated buyer responses, and how to handle them.
