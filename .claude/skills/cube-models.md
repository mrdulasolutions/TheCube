# THE CUBE -- Mental Models Stack

You are executing The Cube with the **Mental Models Stack**. Six universally applicable mental models mapped to a Rubik's cube. The decision or situation goes at the core. The cube rotates through 30 positions -- faces, edges, axes, corners -- producing a comprehensive multi-dimensional analysis.

Each model is a lens. No single model is sufficient. The power is in the collision between models -- where they agree, you have high confidence; where they disagree, you have the real decision. This is the Munger latticework essentials -- the six most universally applicable mental models for any problem in any domain.

These models deliberately overlap with frameworks in other stacks (JTBD is in Marketing, Root Cause is in Coding) -- but here they are applied as general-purpose mental models, not domain-specific tools.

### THE SIX FACES

**FACE 1 -- OODA LOOP (F) -- John Boyd**
Observe-Orient-Decide-Act. The fighter pilot who revolutionized military strategy discovered that victory goes not to the strongest but to whoever cycles through this loop fastest. Orient is the critical step -- your mental model of the situation, shaped by culture, experience, and analysis. The faster you reorient while the adversary is still observing, the more disoriented they become. Applies to business, competition, crisis response, and any domain where tempo matters.
*"He who can handle the quickest rate of change survives."*
Self-prompt: "Where am I in the OODA loop right now -- observing, orienting, deciding, or acting? Am I cycling faster than my competition? What is distorting my orientation -- outdated mental models, cultural bias, incomplete information? What would change if I acted right now instead of analyzing further?"

**FACE 2 -- CIRCLE OF COMPETENCE (B) -- Warren Buffett & Charlie Munger**
Know what you know, know what you don't know, and never confuse the two. The boundary of your circle is more important than its size. Inside the circle, you have genuine expertise and edge. Outside it, you are guessing -- and the world punishes overconfidence outside your circle far more than it rewards ambition. The skill is not expanding the circle, but accurately knowing where the edge is.
*"What an investor needs is the ability to correctly evaluate selected businesses. Note that word 'selected': you don't have to be an expert on every company. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital." -- Buffett*
Self-prompt: "Am I inside my circle of competence on this decision? Where exactly is the boundary? What am I claiming to know that I actually don't? Who has this inside THEIR circle that I should defer to?"

**FACE 3 -- JOBS TO BE DONE (L) -- Clayton Christensen**
People don't buy products -- they hire them to make progress in specific circumstances. Innovation succeeds when it solves a job customers are already trying to do. Stop asking "who is my customer?" and start asking "what job are they hiring my product to do?" The job is functional, emotional, AND social. When you understand the job, you understand what to build.
*"People don't want a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole."*
Self-prompt: "What job is being done here? What progress is someone trying to make? What are they firing to hire this solution? What functional, emotional, and social dimensions does the job have?"

**FACE 4 -- ROOT CAUSE / 5 WHYS (R) -- Taiichi Ohno (Toyota Production System)**
The first explanation is almost always a symptom. Ask "why?" five times and you reach the root cause -- which is usually a process, a decision, or a missing safeguard, not the thing that broke. Fixing symptoms guarantees recurrence. Fixing root causes prevents entire categories of failure. The discipline is refusing to stop at the first "because."
*"Having no problems is the biggest problem of all." -- Ohno*
Self-prompt: "Why did this happen? And why did THAT happen? Have I asked 'why' enough times to reach the root cause? Am I about to fix a symptom instead of the underlying cause?"

**FACE 5 -- LEVERAGE & 80/20 (U) -- Vilfredo Pareto / Richard Koch**
Roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. A small number of inputs drive the vast majority of outputs. In any system -- business, time management, relationships, product features -- a vital few factors matter enormously and the trivial many barely matter at all. The leverage question: what is the 20% that drives 80% of results, and am I spending my time there?
*"For many events, roughly 80% of the effects come from 20% of the causes."*
Self-prompt: "What are the vital few inputs that drive most of the results? Where is the leverage -- the small effort that creates disproportionate impact? What trivial many activities am I spending time on that barely matter?"

**FACE 6 -- OPPORTUNITY COST (D) -- Frederic Bastiat / Economics**
Every choice has a hidden cost -- what you give up by choosing this over the next best alternative. The cost of a decision is not what you spend, but what you can't do because you spent it here. Most people evaluate options in isolation; opportunity cost forces you to evaluate them against each other. The unseen alternative is often more valuable than the visible choice.
*"The art of economics consists in looking not merely at the immediate but at the longer effects of any act or policy; it consists in tracing the consequences of that policy not merely for one group but for all groups." -- Hazlitt*
Self-prompt: "What am I giving up by choosing this? What is the next best alternative? If I spend this time/money/attention here, what can I NOT do? Am I evaluating this option in isolation, or against its alternatives?"

### OPPOSITE FACE PAIRS (Rotation Axes)

- **X-Axis (F<>B):** OODA Loop x Circle of Competence -- **Speed vs Wisdom**. Boyd says cycle faster than your competition -- observe, orient, decide, act before they can react. Munger says stay within what you genuinely understand -- overconfidence outside your circle is fatal. One demands tempo; the other demands restraint. The tension reveals whether this situation rewards moving fast (and risking being wrong) or staying disciplined (and risking being slow). The gap between "act now" and "know what you know" is where most catastrophic decisions live.

- **Y-Axis (U<>D):** Leverage/80-20 x Opportunity Cost -- **Focus vs Trade-offs**. Pareto says find the vital few and pour everything into them -- 20% of effort drives 80% of results. Opportunity Cost says every pour has a price -- what are you NOT doing? One says double down on what works. The other says count what you're sacrificing. The tension reveals whether you should be concentrating harder or diversifying to preserve options you're about to lose.

- **Z-Axis (L<>R):** Jobs to Be Done x Root Cause -- **Demand vs Diagnosis**. Christensen looks forward: what progress are people trying to make? What job needs doing? Ohno looks backward: why did this happen? What root cause created this situation? One asks "what do they need?" The other asks "why did this break?" The tension reveals whether the situation requires understanding what to build (JTBD) or understanding what went wrong (Root Cause) -- and often, the root cause of a failure IS a misunderstood job.

### STEP 2: FACE SCAN (Positions 1-6)

Apply each mental model independently to the situation. 4-8 sentences each, ending with Key Insight.

**[F] Position 1: OODA Loop (Boyd)** -- Where are you in the loop? Are you stuck observing when you should act? What is distorting your orientation? Who is cycling faster?
**[B] Position 2: Circle of Competence (Buffett/Munger)** -- Are you inside your circle on this decision? Where is the boundary? What are you claiming to know that you don't? Who should you defer to?
**[L] Position 3: Jobs to Be Done (Christensen)** -- What job is being done? What progress is someone trying to make? What are the functional, emotional, and social dimensions?
**[R] Position 4: Root Cause / 5 Whys (Ohno)** -- Why does this problem exist? Ask why five times. Are you about to fix a symptom or the root cause?
**[U] Position 5: Leverage & 80/20 (Pareto/Koch)** -- What are the vital few inputs? Where is the leverage point? What trivial many activities are consuming time without impact?
**[D] Position 6: Opportunity Cost (Bastiat)** -- What are you giving up? What is the next best alternative? Are you evaluating this in isolation or against its alternatives?

Display Complexity Assessment after all 6 faces.

### STEP 3: EDGE ROTATIONS (Positions 7-18)

3-5 sentences each. Focus on the emergent cross-insight. End with Key Insight.

#### [F-U] Position 7: "The Tempo Lever" (OODA Loop + Leverage/80-20)
*Boyd's speed imperative meets Pareto's vital few -- cycle fastest on the things that actually matter, ignore the rest entirely.*
**Prompt:** "What is the 20% of the OODA loop that drives 80% of competitive advantage? Where should you cycle fastest, and where is speed wasted on trivial inputs? What is the highest-leverage observation you should be making right now?"

#### [F-R] Position 8: "The Rapid Diagnostic" (OODA Loop + Root Cause)
*Boyd's orient phase demands accurate situational awareness. Ohno's 5 Whys demand you see past symptoms. Together -- orient on root causes, not surface signals.*
**Prompt:** "Is your current orientation based on symptoms or root causes? What happens if you cycle through OODA using the wrong diagnosis? How do you build root cause analysis into your orient step without slowing the loop?"

#### [F-D] Position 9: "The Speed Tax" (OODA Loop + Opportunity Cost)
*Every cycle through the OODA loop costs something -- time, attention, alternatives foreclosed. Acting fast means choosing fast, and every choice kills options.*
**Prompt:** "What options are you foreclosing by acting now? What is the opportunity cost of speed -- and what is the opportunity cost of waiting? Is faster actually better here, or does this decision benefit from deliberation?"

#### [F-L] Position 10: "The Mission Cycle" (OODA Loop + Jobs to Be Done)
*Boyd says orient on the situation. Christensen says orient on the job. Together -- the fastest way to win is to cycle on the right job, not just the right action.*
**Prompt:** "Are you cycling the OODA loop on the right job? What job is actually being done here -- and are you observing-orienting-deciding-acting on that job, or on a job you assumed? What would change if you reoriented around the real job to be done?"

#### [B-U] Position 11: "The Competence Multiplier" (Circle of Competence + Leverage/80-20)
*Munger's circle tells you WHERE you have edge. Pareto tells you to concentrate there ruthlessly. Together -- maximum leverage comes from doubling down inside your circle and ruthlessly pruning everything outside it.*
**Prompt:** "Where does your circle of competence overlap with the highest-leverage activities? Are you spending time on low-leverage tasks outside your circle? What would happen if you only worked on the 20% of activities that fall inside your circle?"

#### [B-R] Position 12: "The Honest Autopsy" (Circle of Competence + Root Cause)
*Munger demands intellectual honesty about what you know. Ohno demands you trace problems to their source. Together -- the root cause of most failures is operating outside your circle and not knowing it.*
**Prompt:** "Is the root cause of this problem that someone operated outside their circle of competence? Did overconfidence in expertise that doesn't exist create this situation? What would an honest audit of competence boundaries reveal?"

#### [B-D] Position 13: "The Knowing No" (Circle of Competence + Opportunity Cost)
*Staying inside your circle means saying no to opportunities outside it. Every no has an opportunity cost -- but Munger argues the cost of saying yes outside your circle is almost always higher.*
**Prompt:** "What opportunities are you passing on by staying inside your circle? Are any of those worth the risk of operating outside it? What is the opportunity cost of competence -- and is it worth paying?"

#### [B-L] Position 14: "The Edge Finder" (Circle of Competence + Jobs to Be Done)
*Munger asks: what do I genuinely know? Christensen asks: what job needs doing? Together -- your real competitive advantage is solving jobs that fall squarely inside your circle that others cannot see.*
**Prompt:** "What jobs to be done fall inside your circle of competence? Are you pursuing jobs that are outside your circle -- jobs someone else is better equipped to solve? What is the job that ONLY you, with YOUR specific expertise, can do?"

#### [U-L] Position 15: "The Leverage Job" (Leverage/80-20 + Jobs to Be Done)
*Pareto says find the vital few. Christensen says find the real job. Together -- the highest-leverage move is solving the job that matters most, not the job that's most visible.*
**Prompt:** "What is the highest-leverage job to be done? Is the most visible job actually the most important one? What 20% of jobs-to-be-done drive 80% of the progress people are trying to make?"

#### [U-R] Position 16: "The Root Lever" (Leverage/80-20 + Root Cause)
*Pareto says a vital few causes drive most effects. Ohno says trace to the root cause. Together -- the ultimate leverage point is the root cause of the vital few.*
**Prompt:** "What is the root cause of the 20% of inputs that drive 80% of results? If you fix the root cause of the highest-leverage problem, how many downstream problems disappear? Where is the single point of maximum leverage?"

#### [D-L] Position 17: "The Hidden Job" (Opportunity Cost + Jobs to Be Done)
*Every job you choose to solve means jobs you choose NOT to solve. The job you're ignoring might be the one that matters most.*
**Prompt:** "What jobs are you NOT doing because you chose this one? Is there a higher-value job you're sacrificing? What is the opportunity cost of solving this job instead of the alternatives?"

#### [D-R] Position 18: "The Prevention Price" (Opportunity Cost + Root Cause)
*Fixing root causes requires investment. Not fixing them guarantees recurring costs. The opportunity cost of prevention vs. the recurring cost of symptoms.*
**Prompt:** "What is the opportunity cost of fixing the root cause now -- time, money, attention diverted from other things? What is the recurring cost of NOT fixing it? Is the prevention price worth paying, or are you better off managing symptoms while you invest elsewhere?"

### STEP 4: AXIS TENSIONS (Positions 19-21)

4-6 sentences each exploring the dialectical tension.

#### [X-Axis] Position 19: "The Decisive Moment" (OODA Loop x Circle of Competence)
*Speed vs Wisdom. When to move fast and when to stay disciplined.*
**Prompt:** "Boyd says act before the competition can react. Munger says never act outside what you genuinely understand. For THIS situation, which is more dangerous -- moving too fast or moving too slow? Is this inside your circle of competence where speed is safe, or outside it where speed is reckless? What would Boyd tell Munger? What would Munger tell Boyd?"

#### [Y-Axis] Position 20: "The Allocation Paradox" (Leverage/80-20 x Opportunity Cost)
*Focus vs Trade-offs. Double down or diversify.*
**Prompt:** "Pareto says pour everything into the vital few. Opportunity Cost says every pour has a price -- count what you're sacrificing. Should you concentrate harder on what's working, or diversify to preserve options? What are you losing by focusing? What are you losing by NOT focusing? Is the 80/20 distribution stable, or are you about to over-concentrate into a position that shifts?"

#### [Z-Axis] Position 21: "The Forward-Backward" (Jobs to Be Done x Root Cause)
*Demand vs Diagnosis. Build forward or fix backward.*
**Prompt:** "Christensen looks forward: what progress needs to happen? Ohno looks backward: why did this break? For THIS situation, is the answer understanding what to build or understanding what went wrong? Are they the same question -- is the root cause of the failure a misunderstood job? Should you be innovating forward or diagnosing backward?"

### STEP 5: CORNER ROTATIONS (Positions 22-29)

3-5 sentences each. Each corner picks one from each opposite pair.

#### [FUL] Position 22: "The Blitz" (OODA Loop + Leverage/80-20 + Jobs to Be Done)
*Maximum speed on the highest-leverage job. Cycle fast, focus on what matters, solve the real job.*
**Prompt:** "You know the job. You know the leverage point. Now cycle the OODA loop as fast as possible on the vital few. What does a blitz look like for this situation -- rapid iteration on the highest-leverage job to be done? What gets sacrificed for speed, and is that acceptable?"

#### [FUR] Position 23: "The Strike" (OODA Loop + Leverage/80-20 + Root Cause)
*Fast action on the root cause of the highest-leverage problem. Surgical speed targeting the source.*
**Prompt:** "You've identified the root cause of the vital few problems. Now act fast. What does a precision strike look like -- OODA cycling on the root cause of the highest-leverage issue? Is the diagnosis solid enough to justify speed?"

#### [FDL] Position 24: "The Bet" (OODA Loop + Opportunity Cost + Jobs to Be Done)
*Fast action with full awareness of what you're giving up to solve this job. Speed is a bet -- every fast move forecloses alternatives.*
**Prompt:** "You're choosing to move fast on this job, which means you're NOT moving fast on other jobs. Is this the right bet? What is the opportunity cost of this cycle? If this job turns out to be wrong, how quickly can you reorient?"

#### [FDR] Position 25: "The Triage" (OODA Loop + Opportunity Cost + Root Cause)
*Fast root cause diagnosis with clear awareness of costs. Triage -- what to fix, what to let go, how fast to move.*
**Prompt:** "You can't fix every root cause. You can't cycle on every problem. Given the opportunity costs, which root cause deserves the fastest OODA cycle? What do you triage out -- and what is the cost of letting those root causes persist?"

#### [BUL] Position 26: "The Sweet Spot" (Circle of Competence + Leverage/80-20 + Jobs to Be Done)
*Maximum wisdom -- the highest-leverage job that falls inside your circle. This is where you have genuine edge AND disproportionate impact.*
**Prompt:** "Where is the sweet spot -- the job to be done that is both inside your circle of competence AND in the vital 20%? This is where you have genuine competitive advantage AND maximum leverage. What does this sweet spot look like for this situation? Are you actually operating there?"

#### [BUR] Position 27: "The Expert Fix" (Circle of Competence + Leverage/80-20 + Root Cause)
*Deep expertise applied to the root cause of the highest-leverage problem. The most disciplined, surgical analysis in the cube.*
**Prompt:** "You're inside your circle. You've found the vital few. You've traced to the root cause. What does expert-level root cause resolution look like? What does someone with genuine competence see that a generalist would miss? Is the root cause inside your circle, or do you need to bring in someone whose circle includes it?"

#### [BDL] Position 28: "The Wise Pass" (Circle of Competence + Opportunity Cost + Jobs to Be Done)
*Knowing when to say no. The job exists, but is it yours to solve? The opportunity cost of doing work outside your circle.*
**Prompt:** "This job needs doing -- but is it YOUR job? What is the opportunity cost of taking on a job outside your circle of competence? Who should be doing this instead? What job should YOU be doing while they do this one? When is the wise move to pass?"

#### [BDR] Position 29: "The Audit" (Circle of Competence + Opportunity Cost + Root Cause)
*Maximum intellectual honesty. Trace the root cause, count the real costs, and be brutally honest about what you actually know.*
**Prompt:** "What is the root cause of this situation, what is the real opportunity cost of each option, and -- most importantly -- are you inside your circle of competence to even make this assessment? This is the most honest corner of the cube. What does a full audit reveal when you remove ego, sunk costs, and wishful thinking?"

### STEP 6: RETURN TO HOME -- Grand Synthesis [Position 30]

Follow the core Cube synthesis protocol:

**6a. Executive Summary** -- 4 lines: the real decision, clearest model insight, biggest risk, key trade-off.
**6b. Model Application Map** -- Which models revealed what. For each of the six models, one sentence on its unique contribution to this analysis.
**6c. Convergence Map** -- Where multiple models agree on the same conclusion. High-confidence findings.
**6d. Divergence Report** -- Where models contradict each other for this situation. These are the real decisions.
**6e. Decision Quality Assessment** -- Based on the analysis: Is this decision inside or outside the circle of competence? Is the OODA tempo appropriate? Has the root cause been identified? Are opportunity costs accounted for? Is leverage being applied to the vital few?
**6f. Leverage Point Identification** -- Rank top 3 leverage points with:
- Which models identified this leverage point
- The root cause it addresses
- The job it serves
- The opportunity cost of NOT acting on it
- Confidence level (based on convergence across models)
**6g. Recommended Action** -- The single best action for THIS specific situation, citing which models support it and which caution against it.
**6h. Model Warnings** -- What each model says could go wrong with the recommended action.

## EXECUTION NOTES

1. **Each model is a lens. No single model is sufficient.** The power is in the collision between models -- where they agree, you have high confidence; where they disagree, you have the real decision.
2. **These are universal models.** They work on any problem -- business, personal, strategic, tactical, creative, technical. Apply them to the specific situation, not in the abstract.
3. **Honesty over comfort.** Circle of Competence demands intellectual honesty. Opportunity Cost demands counting what's unseen. Root Cause demands refusing to stop at symptoms. Do not soften the analysis.
4. **Specificity over generality.** "Consider the opportunity cost" is not an insight. "You are spending 40 hours/week on a channel that produces 5% of revenue while ignoring the channel that produces 60%" is an insight.
5. **The situation is the Core.** Never lose sight of the specific decision, problem, or situation. Every rotation should move closer to a specific, actionable recommendation.
