# THE CUBE -- Founder Stack

You are executing The Cube with the **Founder Stack**. Six proven startup frameworks mapped to a Rubik's cube. The founder's challenge goes at the core. The cube rotates through 30 positions -- faces, edges, axes, corners -- producing a comprehensive multi-dimensional founder analysis.

Each framework sees the startup, the market, and the existential decision from a different angle. The rotations force you out of whatever founder mode you are stuck in.

### THE SIX FACES

**FACE 1 -- EFFECTUATION (F) -- Saras Sarasvathy**
Start with what you have (who you are, what you know, whom you know), not what you want. Expert entrepreneurs don't predict the future -- they negotiate commitments to co-create it. Affordable loss replaces expected return. Leverage surprises instead of avoiding them.
Self-prompt: "What can I do right now with the means I already have? Who can I get a commitment from today? What is the most I can afford to lose?"

**FACE 2 -- DEFINITE OPTIMISM / ZERO TO ONE (B) -- Peter Thiel**
The future is not random -- a specific person with a specific plan can construct it. Seek secrets (truths few agree with). Build a monopoly, not competition. Go from zero to one (new) rather than one to N (copying).
*"What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"*
Self-prompt: "What secret truth am I building on? What is my concrete plan for monopoly? Am I building something genuinely new?"

**FACE 3 -- WARTIME/PEACETIME CEO (L) -- Ben Horowitz**
A startup is either in peacetime (expanding, focus on culture) or wartime (existential threat, must survive). Same CEO behaviors that work in peacetime are catastrophic in wartime, and vice versa.
*"Peacetime CEO knows that proper protocol leads to winning. Wartime CEO violates protocol in order to win."*
Self-prompt: "Is this peacetime or wartime? Am I applying peacetime tactics to a wartime problem? What would a wartime CEO do right now?"

**FACE 4 -- DEFAULT ALIVE / DEFAULT DEAD (R) -- Paul Graham / YC**
At current growth rate and spending, will the startup become profitable before money runs out? Most founders don't know. If default dead, nothing matters except changing that trajectory. Do things that don't scale.
*"The fatal pinch is when you realize you're default dead but lack the time or money to fix it."*
Self-prompt: "At our current growth and burn, do we reach profitability before money runs out? If default dead, what specifically must change?"

**FACE 5 -- STRATEGIC INFLECTION POINTS (U) -- Andy Grove**
A 10X change in any force (competitors, suppliers, customers, technology, regulations) signals an inflection point. Most companies die not from the change itself, but because management recognizes it too late.
*"Only the paranoid survive."*
Self-prompt: "Is there a 10X change happening right now? Am I denying a signal because it threatens my current strategy? What would I do if I knew this was an inflection point?"

**FACE 6 -- BLITZSCALING (D) -- Reid Hoffman**
When a market has network effects or first-scaler advantages, speed matters more than efficiency. Accept chaos, waste, and organizational pain to capture the window. The opposite of lean.
*"If you aren't embarrassed by the first version of your product, you've launched too late."*
Self-prompt: "Does this market reward the first to scale, or the best product? Am I moving too slowly -- or blitzscaling when I should be learning?"

### OPPOSITE FACE PAIRS (Rotation Axes)

- **X-Axis (F<>B):** Effectuation x Definite Optimism -- **Improvisation vs Vision**. Sarasvathy says expert entrepreneurs work with what they have, negotiate commitments, and co-create the future through affordable-loss bets rather than prediction. Thiel says the future is not random -- a specific person with a specific plan and a secret truth can construct it, building monopoly through definite conviction. One builds from the ground up with available means; the other starts with a vision of what must exist and works backward. The tension reveals whether this startup needs flexible opportunism or visionary stubbornness -- and when it is time to switch.

- **Y-Axis (U<>D):** Strategic Inflection Points x Blitzscaling -- **Disruption vs Acceleration**. Grove says a 10X change in competitive forces demands that you slow down, recognize the inflection, and fundamentally rethink your strategy before the shift kills you. Hoffman says when network effects or first-scaler advantages exist, speed is everything -- accept chaos, waste, and organizational pain to capture the window before it closes. One says the ground is shifting so stop and look; the other says the window is closing so run. The tension reveals whether the market rewards the company that reads the shift correctly or the one that moves fastest.

- **Z-Axis (L<>R):** Wartime/Peacetime x Default Alive/Dead -- **Leadership Mode vs Survival Math**. Horowitz says the CEO must correctly diagnose the qualitative context -- peacetime behaviors in wartime are fatal, and wartime ruthlessness in peacetime destroys culture. Graham says forget vibes and run the numbers -- at your current growth and burn, do you reach profitability before the money runs out? One is a leadership instinct call, the other is arithmetic. The tension reveals when the math and the instinct disagree -- and which one the founder should trust.

### STEP 3: EDGE ROTATIONS (Positions 7-18)

3-5 sentences each. Focus on the emergent cross-insight. End with Key Insight.

#### [F-U] Position 7: "The Pivot Sensor" (Effectuation + Strategic Inflection Points)
*Sarasvathy's means-driven improvisation meets Grove's 10X change detection -- the founder who works with what they have notices the ground shifting before the planner does.*
**Prompt:** "When you build from available means, you feel market shifts in real time because you are close to the ground. How does your effectual approach inform whether a strategic inflection point is underway? Are you pivoting toward the shift or away from it?"

#### [F-R] Position 8: "The Bootstrap Equation" (Effectuation + Default Alive/Dead)
*Sarasvathy's affordable-loss thinking meets Graham's survival math -- together they produce the leanest possible path to default alive.*
**Prompt:** "Effectuation says bet only what you can afford to lose. Graham says run the numbers -- are you default alive or dead? Combined: what is the minimum viable path to profitability using only the means you already have? Where does affordable loss become unaffordable?"

#### [F-D] Position 9: "The Scrappy Scaler" (Effectuation + Blitzscaling)
*Sarasvathy says start with what you have. Hoffman says speed is everything. These seem contradictory -- but the best founders scale scrappily, using available means at maximum velocity.*
**Prompt:** "Effectuation says use what you have. Blitzscaling says move faster than is comfortable. Can you blitzscale with available means, or does blitzscaling require resources you do not have? What does 'scrappy speed' look like for this startup?"

#### [F-L] Position 10: "The Adaptive Commander" (Effectuation + Wartime/Peacetime)
*Sarasvathy's flexible, co-creative approach meets Horowitz's wartime/peacetime diagnosis -- the founder who adapts their leadership mode based on available means and current context.*
**Prompt:** "Effectuation is inherently adaptive. Wartime demands focus and ruthlessness. How does the founder who builds from available means behave differently in wartime vs peacetime? Is your current adaptiveness a strength or a lack of conviction?"

#### [B-U] Position 11: "The Cassandra Test" (Definite Optimism + Strategic Inflection Points)
*Thiel's conviction in a definite future meets Grove's warning that 10X changes kill those who recognize them too late -- the question is whether your secret truth survives the inflection.*
**Prompt:** "You have a secret truth and a definite plan. But Grove says inflection points destroy companies that cling to their current strategy. Does your secret truth still hold if the ground shifts 10X? Are you a visionary or a Cassandra -- right about the future but wrong about the timing?"

#### [B-R] Position 12: "The Monopoly Math" (Definite Optimism + Default Alive/Dead)
*Thiel's monopoly ambition meets Graham's survival arithmetic -- the tension between building for dominance and staying alive long enough to get there.*
**Prompt:** "Thiel says build a monopoly. Graham says survive. Can you reach monopoly before the money runs out? If your monopoly plan requires years and your runway is months, which do you sacrifice -- the vision or the timeline? What does 'default alive on the path to monopoly' look like?"

#### [B-D] Position 13: "The Lightning Strike" (Definite Optimism + Blitzscaling)
*Thiel's definite plan meets Hoffman's urgency to scale -- maximum conviction at maximum speed. The founder who knows exactly what to build and builds it as fast as possible.*
**Prompt:** "You have a secret truth and a definite plan (Thiel). The market rewards the first to scale (Hoffman). This is the most aggressive combination -- total conviction at full speed. When is this exactly right? When does speed corrupt the vision?"

#### [B-L] Position 14: "The Wartime Visionary" (Definite Optimism + Wartime/Peacetime)
*Thiel's long-term monopoly vision meets Horowitz's diagnosis that this might be wartime -- the founder who must hold the vision while fighting for survival.*
**Prompt:** "You have a definite vision of the future. But the company is under existential threat. Does wartime demand you abandon the vision to survive, or does the vision give wartime meaning? How does a wartime CEO maintain zero-to-one thinking when the building is on fire?"

#### [U-L] Position 15: "The War Room" (Strategic Inflection Points + Wartime/Peacetime)
*Grove's inflection point is the very thing that triggers Horowitz's wartime. This edge is where the founder recognizes the shift and switches leadership modes.*
**Prompt:** "A strategic inflection point IS the event that turns peacetime into wartime. Have you recognized this? Are you still running peacetime playbooks during an inflection point? What does the War Room look like -- who is in it, what decisions get made, what gets cut?"

#### [U-R] Position 16: "The Survival Radar" (Strategic Inflection Points + Default Alive/Dead)
*Grove's 10X change detection meets Graham's survival arithmetic -- the founder who spots the inflection AND knows exactly how it changes the math.*
**Prompt:** "A 10X change is underway. How does it change your default-alive/dead calculation? Does the inflection make you more default-alive (new opportunity) or more default-dead (market collapse)? What must change in your growth rate or burn rate to survive the shift?"

#### [D-L] Position 17: "The Blitz Commander" (Blitzscaling + Wartime/Peacetime)
*Hoffman's speed imperative meets Horowitz's wartime intensity -- when blitzscaling IS the war, the founder must lead with wartime discipline at peacetime-breaking speed.*
**Prompt:** "Blitzscaling creates internal chaos. Wartime demands ruthless focus. Can you blitzscale AND maintain wartime discipline, or does the speed create its own wartime? What does a wartime CEO do when the enemy is not a competitor but your own scaling dysfunction?"

#### [D-R] Position 18: "The Burn Accelerator" (Blitzscaling + Default Alive/Dead)
*Hoffman says spend to win the market. Graham says survive. This is the most dangerous edge in the cube -- the founder who must decide whether speed or survival comes first.*
**Prompt:** "Blitzscaling deliberately burns cash to capture market. Graham says if you are default dead, nothing else matters. How do you reconcile 'spend to win' with 'survive to fight'? At what burn rate does blitzscaling become suicide? At what growth rate does conservation become surrender?"

## EXECUTION NOTES

1. **Specificity over generality.** "Extend your runway" is not an insight. "Cut the ML team from 4 to 2, ship the rule-based MVP by March, and use the 6 months of saved burn to prove conversion before Series A" is an insight.
2. **Honor the frameworks.** Each face represents a real, researched framework by its creator. Apply it as Sarasvathy, Thiel, Horowitz, Graham, Grove, or Hoffman intended, then let the cross-analysis find the synthesis.
3. **The startup is the Core.** Never lose sight of the specific stage, runway, team, traction, and market. Every rotation should move closer to a specific, executable founder decision.
4. **Survival is non-negotiable.** Default-dead status overrides all other analysis. If the math says the company dies in 4 months, every rotation must address that reality before anything else.
