# THE CUBE -- Enterprise Stack

You are executing The Cube with the **Enterprise Stack**. Six proven enterprise governance, risk, and organizational design frameworks mapped to a Rubik's cube. The enterprise challenge goes at the core. The cube rotates through 30 positions -- faces, edges, axes, corners -- producing a comprehensive multi-dimensional enterprise analysis.

Each framework sees the organization, the risk landscape, and the governance challenge from a different altitude. The rotations force you out of whatever management orthodoxy or institutional bias you are stuck in.

### THE SIX FACES

**FACE 1 -- CYNEFIN (F) -- Dave Snowden**
Classifies the decision domain as Simple, Complicated, Complex, or Chaotic. Each domain demands a fundamentally different decision approach -- best practice, good practice, emergent practice, or novel practice. Most enterprise failures stem from leaders applying ordered-domain practices (analyze-plan-execute) to complex or chaotic situations where cause and effect can only be understood in retrospect.
*"The way we manage depends on the nature of the system we are managing."*
Self-prompt: "What domain is this problem actually in -- ordered, complex, or chaotic? Am I applying the right decision approach for that domain?"

**FACE 2 -- COSO ERM (B) -- Treadway Commission**
Enterprise Risk Management integrating risk appetite with strategy and performance across the entire organization. Risk is not a checklist -- it is embedded in every strategic choice. The framework forces the question: is our governance structure capable of seeing what is going wrong before it compounds? If risk management is divorced from strategy-setting, both fail.
*"Risk is managed in the context of strategy and objectives."*
Self-prompt: "What risks are embedded in the strategy itself? Does our governance structure have the visibility, authority, and culture to detect and respond?"

**FACE 3 -- GALBRAITH STAR MODEL (L) -- Jay Galbraith**
Organizational effectiveness depends on the alignment of five design levers: Strategy, Structure, Processes, Rewards, and People. Misalignment between any two creates friction the organization experiences as dysfunction. Most problems that surface as execution failures are actually alignment failures -- the organization's design is actively working against its stated objectives.
*"Organization design is the deliberate process of configuring structures, processes, reward systems, and people practices to create an effective organization."*
Self-prompt: "Which of the five design levers are misaligned? Where is the organization's design actively working against its stated objective?"

**FACE 4 -- KOTTER 8-STEP CHANGE (R) -- John Kotter**
Large-scale organizational change fails at predictable points: no urgency, no guiding coalition, no compelling vision, under-communication, failure to remove barriers, no short-term wins, premature victory declarations, or failure to anchor changes in culture. The failure is almost always upstream of where the symptom appears -- what looks like resistance is usually the consequence of a skipped step.
*"The rate of change is not going to slow down anytime soon. If anything, competition in most industries will probably speed up even more in the next few decades."*
Self-prompt: "Which step in the change sequence has been skipped or faked? Is there real urgency, a real coalition, and a real vision -- or performative versions?"

**FACE 5 -- STAKEHOLDER SALIENCE (U) -- Mitchell, Agle & Wood**
Stakeholder salience depends on three attributes: Power (ability to impose will), Legitimacy (socially accepted and expected claim), and Urgency (time sensitivity of the claim). Stakeholders possessing all three are "definitive" and cannot be ignored. Most decisions fail not from lack of analysis but from misreading who actually matters -- giving attention to stakeholders who lack salience while ignoring those who hold it.
*"Who and what really count."*
Self-prompt: "Who are the definitive stakeholders -- those with power, legitimacy, AND urgency? Who has been given attention they don't warrant? Who is being ignored?"

**FACE 6 -- COBIT GOVERNANCE (D) -- ISACA**
Separates governance (evaluate, direct, monitor) from management (plan, build, run, monitor). Most enterprise dysfunction occurs when these are conflated -- when executives manage instead of governing, or when managers are forced to govern without authority. Control objectives must be defined, measured, and enforced -- not merely documented.
*"Governance ensures that stakeholder needs, conditions, and options are evaluated to determine balanced, agreed-on enterprise objectives."*
Self-prompt: "Is this a governance failure or a management failure? Are control objectives defined, measured, and enforced -- or are they theater?"

### OPPOSITE FACE PAIRS (Rotation Axes)

- **X-Axis (F<>B):** Cynefin x COSO ERM -- **Complexity vs Control**. Snowden's Cynefin teaches that the nature of the system determines the valid decision approach -- in complex domains, you probe-sense-respond rather than analyze-plan-execute. COSO ERM assumes risks can be identified, categorized, and controlled through structured frameworks and governance processes. The tension reveals a critical enterprise question: when is imposing structure the right move, and when does the illusion of control create more risk than it mitigates? In ordered domains, COSO's rigor is essential. In complex domains, over-specification of risk controls can suppress the very experimentation needed to navigate safely.

- **Y-Axis (U<>D):** Stakeholder Salience x COBIT -- **Politics vs Process**. Mitchell, Agle & Wood's salience model maps the political reality of who actually has power, legitimacy, and urgency -- the informal authority structure that determines what really happens. COBIT defines the formal governance architecture -- roles, control objectives, maturity models -- that determines what is supposed to happen. Both are true simultaneously. Formal governance is necessary but insufficient without understanding political dynamics; political savvy without formal structure produces fiefdoms. The tension reveals where formal processes are being subverted by political reality and where political dynamics are being ignored by process designers.

- **Z-Axis (L<>R):** Galbraith Star Model x Kotter 8-Step -- **Design vs Change**. Galbraith diagnoses the right organizational design -- what should the structure, processes, rewards, and people configuration look like? Kotter maps how to get there -- building urgency, forming coalitions, communicating vision, and anchoring change. Design without change management is insight without action: you know the right structure but cannot reach it. Change without design is motion without direction: you generate momentum with no coherent destination. The tension reveals whether the organization's primary deficit is knowing what to become or knowing how to get there.

### STEP 3: EDGE ROTATIONS (Positions 7-18)

3-5 sentences each. Focus on the emergent cross-insight. End with Key Insight.

#### [F-U] Position 7: "The Power Map" (Cynefin + Stakeholder Salience)
*Snowden's domain classification meets Mitchell's salience model -- the nature of the system determines which stakeholders can actually influence outcomes, and which are operating on false assumptions about how the system works.*
**Prompt:** "Does the domain type change which stakeholders are actually salient? In a complex domain, do the 'definitive' stakeholders even have the power they think they have? Who gains or loses salience as the domain shifts?"

#### [F-R] Position 8: "The Burning Platform" (Cynefin + Kotter)
*Snowden warns that complex systems cannot be changed through linear plans. Kotter insists on sequenced, escalating change steps. The collision reveals when a structured change approach works and when the system demands adaptive, probe-sense-respond leadership instead.*
**Prompt:** "Is this change happening in an ordered domain where Kotter's sequence applies, or a complex domain where sequenced change will fail? How do you create urgency for a problem whose cause-and-effect pattern is not yet visible?"

#### [F-D] Position 9: "The Control Illusion" (Cynefin + COBIT)
*Snowden's framework warns that in complex and chaotic domains, traditional governance controls create a dangerous illusion of order. COBIT's governance-management separation assumes controllability. The collision exposes where formal controls are theater -- reassuring but not protecting.*
**Prompt:** "Which COBIT control objectives are valid in this domain type, and which are governance theater? In a complex or chaotic domain, what does meaningful governance actually look like if traditional controls do not work?"

#### [F-L] Position 10: "The Misfit" (Cynefin + Galbraith)
*Snowden identifies the domain; Galbraith identifies the organizational design. The collision reveals whether the organization is designed for the domain it actually operates in -- or designed for the domain its leaders wish they were in.*
**Prompt:** "Is this organization designed for an ordered domain but operating in a complex one? Which Star Model levers need to change to match the actual domain? What happens when the structure assumes predictability but the environment is emergent?"

#### [B-U] Position 11: "The Blind Spot" (COSO ERM + Stakeholder Salience)
*COSO's risk framework depends on information flowing upward to governance. Mitchell's salience model reveals who controls that information flow. The collision exposes the political filtering of risk -- which risks are reported, which are suppressed, and by whom.*
**Prompt:** "Which definitive stakeholders control the flow of risk information? What risks are being suppressed or minimized because of who benefits from the current risk posture? Where is political salience overriding risk visibility?"

#### [B-R] Position 12: "The Transformation Risk" (COSO ERM + Kotter)
*COSO identifies the risks embedded in strategy. Kotter maps the change required to execute that strategy. The collision reveals the risks of the transformation itself -- the execution risks that neither framework addresses alone.*
**Prompt:** "What risks does the change process itself introduce? Which of Kotter's eight steps, if skipped, creates the greatest risk exposure? Is the risk appetite defined for the transition state, not just the end state?"

#### [B-D] Position 13: "The Audit Trail" (COSO ERM + COBIT)
*COSO's enterprise risk management meets COBIT's governance-management separation -- the most structurally rigorous pairing in the cube. Together they ask: is the control environment actually controlling anything, or is it producing documentation that nobody reads?*
**Prompt:** "Does the governance structure (COBIT) have the authority and information to manage the risks COSO has identified? Are control objectives producing real assurance or compliance artifacts? Where is the audit trail disconnected from actual risk reduction?"

#### [B-L] Position 14: "The Risk Architecture" (COSO ERM + Galbraith)
*COSO identifies enterprise risks. Galbraith's Star Model reveals whether the organization is designed to manage them. The collision exposes structural risk -- risk created by the organization's own design misalignment.*
**Prompt:** "Which risks exist because of how the organization is designed, not because of external threats? Are the five Star Model levers aligned to support risk management, or are they actively creating the risks COSO has flagged?"

#### [U-L] Position 15: "The Influence Architecture" (Stakeholder Salience + Galbraith)
*Mitchell's salience mapping reveals who has power. Galbraith's Star Model reveals what the structure rewards. The collision exposes whether the organizational design empowers the right stakeholders or structurally amplifies the wrong ones.*
**Prompt:** "Does the organizational design (structure, rewards, processes) give power to the stakeholders with legitimate claims? Or does it structurally empower stakeholders whose salience comes from position rather than contribution?"

#### [U-R] Position 16: "The Coalition Builder" (Stakeholder Salience + Kotter)
*Mitchell identifies who has power, legitimacy, and urgency. Kotter requires a guiding coalition with enough combined authority to lead change. The collision reveals whether the change coalition actually represents the definitive stakeholders -- or has assembled the willing instead of the powerful.*
**Prompt:** "Does the guiding coalition include the definitive stakeholders -- or just the enthusiastic ones? Which stakeholders with real power have been excluded? What happens when the coalition lacks the salience to overcome resistance?"

#### [D-L] Position 17: "The Operating Model" (COBIT + Galbraith)
*COBIT defines what governance should evaluate, direct, and monitor. Galbraith's Star Model defines how the organization is actually built. The collision reveals the gap between governance intent and organizational reality -- where the operating model undermines the governance architecture.*
**Prompt:** "Does the organizational design (Star Model) support or undermine the governance objectives (COBIT)? Where do structure, rewards, or processes create incentives that work against governance controls? What operating model changes would make governance effective rather than aspirational?"

#### [D-R] Position 18: "The Change Controls" (COBIT + Kotter)
*COBIT's governance framework meets Kotter's change model -- formal controls applied to the transformation process itself. The collision reveals whether change management is governed or ungoverned, and whether governance accelerates or paralyzes the change.*
**Prompt:** "Is the change process itself governed, or is it operating outside normal controls? Does governance accelerate the transformation or create bureaucratic drag that kills momentum? What control objectives are needed for the transition state?"

## EXECUTION NOTES

1. **Domain diagnosis first.** Before recommending any governance structure, change approach, or stakeholder strategy -- classify the domain. The single most common enterprise error is applying ordered-domain solutions to complex-domain problems.
2. **Honor the frameworks.** Each face represents a peer-reviewed, field-tested framework. Apply it as its creators intended, then let the cross-analysis find the synthesis. Do not flatten Cynefin into a simple 2x2 or reduce COSO to a compliance checklist.
3. **The organization is the Core.** Never lose sight of the specific enterprise context -- industry, scale, governance maturity, regulatory environment. Every rotation should move closer to a specific, executable governance or organizational action.
4. **Name the political reality.** Enterprise analysis that ignores stakeholder dynamics is academic. Identify who benefits from the status quo, who controls information flow, and who has the power to block or enable change.
5. **Distinguish governance from management.** Every recommendation should be clear about whether it is a governance action (evaluate, direct, monitor) or a management action (plan, build, run). Conflating them is the failure mode this stack exists to prevent.
