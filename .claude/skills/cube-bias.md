# THE CUBE -- Bias Stack (Decision Trap Stack)

You are executing The Cube with the **Bias Stack**. Six critical cognitive biases mapped to a Rubik's cube. The decision goes at the core. The cube rotates through 30 positions -- faces, edges, axes, corners -- producing a comprehensive cognitive audit that reveals where your thinking is compromised.

Each bias distorts judgment from a different angle. The rotations force you to confront blind spots that feel invisible precisely because they are yours.

### THE SIX FACES

**FACE 1 -- INCENTIVE-CAUSED BIAS (F) -- Charlie Munger**
People respond to incentives, not intentions. When someone's compensation depends on believing something, they will believe it with total sincerity. Never ask a barber if you need a haircut. Map the incentive structure behind every recommendation, decision, and "expert opinion" to see who benefits from the conclusion.
*"Never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives."*
Self-prompt: "Whose incentives are shaping this decision? Who benefits from this conclusion? If I follow the money, does the recommendation still make sense?"

**FACE 2 -- AVAILABILITY BIAS (B) -- Amos Tversky & Daniel Kahneman**
We judge probability by how easily examples come to mind, not by actual frequency. Vivid, recent, emotional events dominate our thinking while invisible, statistical, slow-moving risks are ignored. The news you read shapes your risk model more than reality does.
*"What you see is all there is." -- Kahneman (WYSIATI)*
Self-prompt: "Am I weighting this because it's likely, or because it's vivid? What risks am I ignoring because they don't make headlines? What data would I need to see to replace my gut estimate?"

**FACE 3 -- SOCIAL PROOF (L) -- Robert Cialdini**
When uncertain, people look to what others are doing. The larger the crowd, the stronger the pull. Social proof is a shortcut that works most of the time -- until everyone is following everyone else off a cliff. Herding amplifies both wisdom and stupidity.
*"We view a behavior as more correct in a given situation to the degree that we see others performing it."*
Self-prompt: "Am I doing this because it's right, or because everyone else is? What would I decide if nobody else had decided yet? Is the crowd a signal or a trap?"

**FACE 4 -- ANCHORING (R) -- Amos Tversky & Daniel Kahneman**
The first number, frame, or reference point you encounter disproportionately influences all subsequent judgment -- even when the anchor is arbitrary or irrelevant. Negotiations, valuations, estimates, and decisions are all distorted by whatever number arrived first.
*"People make estimates by starting from an initial value that is adjusted to yield the final answer. Adjustments are typically insufficient."*
Self-prompt: "What was the first number or frame I encountered? Is it distorting my judgment? What would my estimate be if I started from a completely different anchor?"

**FACE 5 -- LOSS AVERSION (U) -- Daniel Kahneman & Amos Tversky (Prospect Theory)**
Losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. This asymmetry causes irrational risk avoidance when protecting gains and irrational risk-seeking when facing losses. Most bad decisions are driven by the fear of losing what you already have.
*"Losses loom larger than gains."*
Self-prompt: "Am I making this decision to gain something, or to avoid losing something? If I reframe the loss as a cost, does the decision change? Am I holding onto something failing because letting go feels like losing?"

**FACE 6 -- CONFIRMATION BIAS (D) -- Peter Wason / Francis Bacon**
We seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms what we already believe. Disconfirming evidence is ignored, reinterpreted, or forgotten. The smarter you are, the better you are at constructing arguments for what you already think.
*"The human understanding, once it has adopted an opinion, collects any instances that confirm it." -- Francis Bacon*
Self-prompt: "What evidence would change my mind? Am I seeking it, or only looking for evidence that supports what I already believe? What would someone who disagrees with me point to?"

### OPPOSITE FACE PAIRS (Rotation Axes)

- **X-Axis (F<>B):** Incentive-Caused Bias x Availability Bias -- **Structural Distortion vs Cognitive Distortion**. Incentive bias corrupts through the environment -- who profits from this conclusion? Availability bias corrupts through cognition -- what's vivid vs what's probable? One is external manipulation; the other is internal malfunction. The tension reveals whether the signal is being distorted before it reaches you, or whether you are distorting it after.

- **Y-Axis (U<>D):** Loss Aversion x Confirmation Bias -- **Fear-Driven Thinking vs Belief-Driven Thinking**. Loss aversion makes decisions from fear of losing what you have. Confirmation bias makes decisions from attachment to what you believe. One distorts through emotion; the other through cognition. The tension reveals whether you are protecting a position because you are afraid to lose it, or because you only see evidence that supports keeping it.

- **Z-Axis (L<>R):** Social Proof x Anchoring -- **External Influence vs Internal Framing**. Social proof distorts by what everyone else is doing (crowd following). Anchoring distorts by what number or frame arrived first (initial reference point). One pulls you toward the herd; the other pins you to an arbitrary starting point. The tension reveals whether your judgment is being shaped by the crowd or by the first thing you saw.

### STEP 3: EDGE ROTATIONS (Positions 7-18)

3-5 sentences each. Focus on the emergent cross-insight where two biases collide. End with Key Insight. Name the specific bias instances at work.

#### [F-U] Position 7: "The Golden Handcuffs" (Incentive-Caused Bias + Loss Aversion)
*Munger's incentive mapping meets Kahneman's loss aversion -- you are paid to keep what you have, and terrified to lose it. Double lock on the status quo.*
**Prompt:** "Are the incentives structured to reward holding on? Is someone profiting from your fear of loss? When 'stay the course' is both the incentive-aligned AND the loss-averse choice, how do you know if it is actually right?"

#### [F-R] Position 8: "The Rigged Ruler" (Incentive-Caused Bias + Anchoring)
*Someone set the anchor, and they had a reason. Munger asks who profits; Kahneman asks what number arrived first. Together -- the anchor was not accidental.*
**Prompt:** "Who set the first number or frame? Did they benefit from where the anchor landed? Is the 'reasonable range' of this decision built on a starting point someone chose for their own advantage?"

#### [F-D] Position 9: "The Echo Paycheck" (Incentive-Caused Bias + Confirmation Bias)
*You are paid to believe something, and you only see evidence that confirms it. Munger's incentive corruption meets Bacon's confirmation trap -- sincere delusion funded by self-interest.*
**Prompt:** "Are you seeking confirming evidence for a conclusion your incentives prefer? If your compensation structure changed, would you suddenly notice disconfirming data you currently ignore?"

#### [F-L] Position 10: "The Industry Consensus" (Incentive-Caused Bias + Social Proof)
*Everyone in the industry does it this way -- and everyone in the industry profits from doing it this way. Munger's incentive lens meets Cialdini's herd: is the consensus real, or is it a cartel of aligned self-interest?*
**Prompt:** "Is 'everyone does it this way' actually evidence of correctness, or evidence that everyone shares the same incentive structure? What would an outsider with no financial stake decide?"

#### [B-U] Position 11: "The Vivid Fear" (Availability Bias + Loss Aversion)
*A dramatic loss event is vivid in memory, making it feel far more probable than it is. Kahneman's availability heuristic inflates the fear; prospect theory doubles it. Together -- paralyzing overestimation of rare catastrophes.*
**Prompt:** "Is the feared loss actually likely, or just easy to imagine because of a vivid example? What is the base rate of this bad outcome? Are you paying a massive premium to avoid a risk that is statistically tiny?"

#### [B-R] Position 12: "The Phantom Number" (Availability Bias + Anchoring)
*The most vivid example becomes the anchor. Tversky and Kahneman's own research links these two directly -- whatever comes to mind first sets the reference point, and adjustment from there is always insufficient.*
**Prompt:** "Is your anchor based on data, or on the most memorable example you can recall? What would the number be if you could not remember any specific case and had to work only from base rates?"

#### [B-D] Position 13: "The Selective Memory" (Availability Bias + Confirmation Bias)
*You remember the examples that confirm your belief, and those vivid memories make the belief feel even more certain. Availability feeds confirmation, which feeds availability -- a self-reinforcing loop of false certainty.*
**Prompt:** "Which examples come to mind easily? Do they all support your current view? What disconfirming examples might exist but fail to come to mind because they are less vivid, less emotional, less recent?"

#### [B-L] Position 14: "The Trending Panic" (Availability Bias + Social Proof)
*Something vivid happens, and everyone reacts. The crowd's reaction makes it feel even more vivid. Availability and social proof create contagion -- not because the risk is real, but because it is visible and shared.*
**Prompt:** "Is this actually a crisis, or is it a vivid event amplified by everyone reacting to each other? If you turned off all external signals -- news, social media, group chat -- what would the data alone tell you?"

#### [U-L] Position 15: "The Stampede" (Loss Aversion + Social Proof)
*Everyone is running for the exits, and loss aversion says you cannot afford to be left behind. Cialdini's herd meets Kahneman's asymmetric fear -- the most dangerous combination in markets, organizations, and group decisions.*
**Prompt:** "Is everyone else panicking because the threat is real, or is the panic itself the only threat? If you were the only person making this decision in isolation, would you still feel the same urgency to act?"

#### [U-R] Position 16: "The Sunk Cost Trap" (Loss Aversion + Anchoring)
*You anchored to what you already invested, and loss aversion refuses to write it off. The original price, the original timeline, the original plan -- all anchors that make you keep throwing good money after bad.*
**Prompt:** "Are you anchored to what you already spent? If you had not yet invested anything, would you start this from scratch today? What is the decision worth going forward, ignoring everything already sunk?"

#### [D-L] Position 17: "The Filter Bubble" (Confirmation Bias + Social Proof)
*You surround yourself with people who think like you. Their agreement confirms your beliefs. Your beliefs select for their company. Wason's confirmation trap and Cialdini's social proof create a sealed reality.*
**Prompt:** "Are the people who agree with you actually independent validators, or did you select them because they agree? What would happen if you presented this decision to people outside your usual circle?"

#### [D-R] Position 18: "The Loaded Question" (Confirmation Bias + Anchoring)
*The question was framed to get the answer you wanted. The anchor was set to confirm the conclusion. When you control both the starting point and the search filter, you can prove anything to yourself.*
**Prompt:** "Did you frame the question in a way that predetermined the answer? If someone hostile to your position framed the same question differently, what anchor would they set, and what would they find?"

### STEP 4: AXIS TENSIONS (Positions 19-21)

4-6 sentences each exploring the dialectical tension between opposite faces.

#### [X-Axis] Position 19: "The Corrupted Signal" (Incentive-Caused Bias x Availability Bias)
*Structural Distortion vs Cognitive Distortion. Is the signal bad, or is the receiver broken?*
**Prompt:** "Munger says someone may be corrupting the information before it reaches you -- follow the money. Kahneman says your own brain is corrupting it after -- follow the vividness. For THIS decision, where is the primary distortion? Is someone feeding you bad data for profit, or are you misreading good data because of what is vivid? Can you distinguish between information that was designed to mislead and information you are naturally misprocessing?"

#### [Y-Axis] Position 20: "The Fortress of Belief" (Loss Aversion x Confirmation Bias)
*Fear-Driven Thinking vs Belief-Driven Thinking. Emotional armor or intellectual armor?*
**Prompt:** "Loss aversion makes you afraid to let go of a position because it hurts to lose. Confirmation bias makes you unable to see why you should let go because you only process supporting evidence. Together they build an impenetrable fortress: you are emotionally locked in AND intellectually sealed off. For THIS decision -- which wall do you need to breach first? If you could eliminate the fear of loss, would the evidence actually support changing course? If you could see all evidence clearly, would you still be afraid?"

#### [Z-Axis] Position 21: "The Invisible Prison" (Social Proof x Anchoring)
*External Influence vs Internal Framing. The crowd's voice or the first voice?*
**Prompt:** "Social proof says you are following the herd -- what everyone else is doing shapes your choice. Anchoring says you are following the first number -- the initial frame shapes your estimate. For THIS decision, which force is stronger? Are you anchored to an initial number and then using the crowd to confirm it? Or did the crowd set the anchor in the first place? If you could reset both -- ignore the crowd and throw out the first number -- what would you decide from scratch?"

### STEP 5: CORNER ROTATIONS (Positions 22-29)

3-5 sentences each. Each corner combines three biases from different axis pairs to triangulate a compound distortion pattern. Name the specific instances operating in THIS decision.

#### [FUL] Position 22: "The Loyalty Trap" (Incentive-Caused Bias + Loss Aversion + Social Proof)
*You are paid to stay, afraid to leave, and everyone around you is staying too. Three forces locking you into the status quo.*
**Prompt:** "The incentives reward loyalty, loss aversion punishes departure, and everyone else is staying. Are you making a decision or are you trapped? What would it take for someone in this exact position to actually leave? Is there any signal strong enough to break through all three locks?"

#### [FUR] Position 23: "The Negotiation Fog" (Incentive-Caused Bias + Loss Aversion + Anchoring)
*Someone set the anchor to benefit themselves, you are afraid of losing your position, and the sunk cost keeps you at the table. The classic bad deal you cannot walk away from.*
**Prompt:** "The anchor was set by someone with incentives. Loss aversion keeps you from walking away from what you already invested. The sunk cost fallacy is anchoring you to the past. What would a cold, new entrant with no history and no stake offer for this deal today?"

#### [FDL] Position 24: "The True Believer" (Incentive-Caused Bias + Confirmation Bias + Social Proof)
*You are paid to believe, you only see evidence that confirms, and everyone around you believes too. This is how entire industries sustain shared delusions for decades.*
**Prompt:** "Is this belief supported by evidence, or supported by an ecosystem of people who all profit from maintaining it? Name one person with no financial stake and no social pressure who independently reached this same conclusion. If you cannot, what does that tell you?"

#### [FDR] Position 25: "The Rigged Game" (Incentive-Caused Bias + Confirmation Bias + Anchoring)
*The benchmark was set by someone who profits from it, you only see evidence that meets the benchmark, and the benchmark itself was never questioned. The game was rigged before you sat down.*
**Prompt:** "Who set the standard, metric, or benchmark being used? Do they benefit from where it was set? Are you only noticing data that confirms the benchmark is correct? What would an adversarial audit of the metric itself reveal?"

#### [BUL] Position 26: "The Panic Herd" (Availability Bias + Loss Aversion + Social Proof)
*A vivid catastrophe is in the headlines, everyone is reacting, and you cannot afford to be the one who did not act. This is how bank runs, market crashes, and organizational stampedes begin.*
**Prompt:** "Is the threat real or just vivid? Is everyone reacting to the threat, or to each other's reactions? If this were a slow-moving, undramatic version of the same risk, would anyone be acting at all? What is the actual base rate of this catastrophe?"

#### [BUR] Position 27: "The Recency Anchor" (Availability Bias + Loss Aversion + Anchoring)
*The most recent vivid event becomes the anchor for all future risk estimates, and loss aversion amplifies the fear. You are now calibrated to the last disaster, not to the actual probability distribution.*
**Prompt:** "Is your risk estimate based on the last thing that happened, or on the full distribution of outcomes? Are you anchored to a recent loss event? What was your risk estimate BEFORE the vivid event, and was that estimate actually more accurate?"

#### [BDL] Position 28: "The Narrative Lock" (Availability Bias + Confirmation Bias + Social Proof)
*A compelling story comes to mind easily, it confirms your existing belief, and everyone around you is telling the same story. The narrative feels like truth because it is vivid, confirming, and unanimous -- but none of those are evidence.*
**Prompt:** "What is the dominant narrative? Is it true, or is it vivid, self-confirming, and socially reinforced? What counternarrative exists that is less dramatic, less popular, but potentially more accurate? What would a boring, data-driven analysis find?"

#### [BDR] Position 29: "The Fixed Frame" (Availability Bias + Confirmation Bias + Anchoring)
*The most available example set the anchor, confirmation bias keeps you from adjusting, and every new data point is interpreted through the original frame. You are stuck in the first story you heard.*
**Prompt:** "What was the first version of this situation you encountered? Has your understanding actually updated since then, or have you been interpreting all subsequent information through that original frame? If you encountered this situation for the first time today, with all current data but no prior exposure, what would you conclude?"

### STEP 6: RETURN TO HOME -- Grand Synthesis [Position 30]

Follow the core Cube synthesis protocol, adapted for cognitive audit:

**6a. Cognitive Audit Summary** -- 4 lines: the real decision trap, the most dangerous bias operating, the most overlooked bias, and the key interaction effect between biases.
**6b. Bias Interaction Map** -- How the biases compound and reinforce each other in this specific decision. Which pairs amplify each other? Where does one bias create the conditions for another?
**6c. Convergence Map** -- Where multiple positions agree on the same distortion. If several rotations all point to the same bias, it is probably the dominant one.
**6d. Divergence Report** -- Which biases pull in different directions. Where does one bias say "act" and another says "wait"?
**6e. Blind Spot Report** -- What only one rotation revealed. The single-rotation insights are often the most important because they are the least obvious.
**6f. Decision Quality Score** -- Rate the decision's vulnerability across dimensions:
- Incentive contamination (low/medium/high)
- Information quality (base-rate grounded vs. availability-driven)
- Independence of judgment (own analysis vs. herd-following)
- Frame integrity (self-set vs. externally anchored)
- Emotional neutrality (gain-seeking vs. loss-avoiding)
- Evidence completeness (disconfirming evidence sought vs. confirming only)
**6g. Debiased Recommendation** -- The decision you would make if you could strip away every bias identified in the audit. Includes:
- What the decision looks like without the three strongest biases operating
- What information you would need to gather before deciding
- What structural changes would reduce bias exposure (e.g., blind evaluation, independent estimate, pre-commitment)
- The single most important debiasing action to take right now
**6h. Final Verdict** -- The single most dangerous cognitive trap in THIS specific decision, and the one intervention most likely to improve decision quality.

## EXECUTION NOTES

1. **Name the specific instance, not just the category.** "Confirmation bias is present" is not an insight. "You are selectively citing the three case studies that support expansion while ignoring the seven that show contraction failed in similar markets" is an insight.
2. **Honor the researchers.** Each face represents real, peer-reviewed cognitive science. Apply it as the researchers documented it, then let the cross-analysis find compound effects.
3. **The decision is the Core.** Never lose sight of the specific decision, its stakes, and its context. Every rotation should move closer to a specific, actionable debiasing intervention.
4. **This is a cognitive audit, not a lecture.** The goal is not to explain what biases are -- the user presumably knows. The goal is to find the specific, named instances of each bias operating in THIS decision and show how they interact.
5. **Debiasing actions must be structural, not aspirational.** "Try to be less biased" is worthless. "Get a valuation from someone who does not get paid if the deal closes" is actionable.
