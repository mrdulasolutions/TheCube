# THE CUBE -- Systems Stack

You are executing The Cube with the **Systems Stack**. Six foundational systems thinking frameworks mapped to a Rubik's cube. The system under analysis goes at the core. The cube rotates through 30 positions -- faces, edges, axes, corners -- producing a comprehensive multi-dimensional systems analysis.

Each framework sees the system's structure, behavior, and dynamics from a different angle. The rotations force you out of whatever mental model you are stuck in -- linear thinking, blame loops, symptomatic fixes -- and into circular, structural, systemic sight.

### THE SIX FACES

**FACE 1 -- FEEDBACK LOOPS (F) -- Donella Meadows**
Every system is driven by reinforcing loops (positive feedback -- amplify change) and balancing loops (negative feedback -- resist change). Identify which loops dominate and you understand why the system behaves as it does. Most interventions fail because they fight a balancing loop or ignore a reinforcing one. The system's behavior IS its loops.
*"The least obvious part of the system, its function or purpose, is often the most crucial determinant of the system's behavior."*
Self-prompt: "What reinforcing loops are driving growth or decline? What balancing loops are maintaining the status quo? Which loop dominates right now, and what would flip that dominance?"

**FACE 2 -- LEVERAGE POINTS (B) -- Donella Meadows**
Not all interventions are equal. Meadows identified 12 leverage points ranked by effectiveness -- from adjusting parameters (weakest) to changing the system's goals or paradigm (strongest). Most people push at low-leverage points (more budget, more staff). The highest leverage is often counterintuitive and invisible from inside the system.
*"People know intuitively where leverage points are, but they push them in the wrong direction."*
Self-prompt: "Where am I pushing in this system? Is it a high-leverage or low-leverage intervention? What would happen if I intervened at the level of rules, goals, or paradigm instead of parameters?"

**FACE 3 -- SYSTEM ARCHETYPES (L) -- Peter Senge**
Systems exhibit recurring patterns of behavior -- archetypes -- that appear across domains. "Shifting the Burden" (short-term fix undermines long-term capacity), "Limits to Growth" (reinforcing loop hits balancing constraint), "Tragedy of the Commons" (shared resource depleted by individual incentives), "Fixes that Fail" (solution worsens the original problem after delay). Recognizing the archetype tells you what happens next.
*"Today's problems come from yesterday's solutions."*
Self-prompt: "Which system archetype am I looking at? Is this Shifting the Burden? Limits to Growth? Tragedy of the Commons? What does the archetype predict happens next?"

**FACE 4 -- CAUSAL LOOP MAPPING (R) -- John Sterman**
Draw the actual causal relationships with arrows showing direction and polarity (same/opposite). Include delays. Follow chains. The map reveals circular causation that linear thinking misses. Most problems persist because people only see one part of a loop and miss the feedback that maintains the problem.
*"We are not guilty of inaction. We are guilty of action based on an incomplete mental model."*
Self-prompt: "Can I draw the causal loop? What causes what? Where are the delays? What circular causation am I missing because I'm only seeing a linear chain?"

**FACE 5 -- EMERGENCE & TIPPING POINTS (U) -- Stuart Kauffman / Malcolm Gladwell**
Systems exhibit emergent properties that cannot be predicted from their components. Small changes accumulate until a tipping point flips the system to a new state. Critical mass, phase transitions, network effects, viral cascades -- the system looks stable until suddenly it isn't. The interesting behavior is not in the parts but in their interaction.
*"The tipping point is that magic moment when an idea, trend, or social behavior crosses a threshold, tips, and spreads like wildfire." -- Gladwell*
Self-prompt: "Is this system near a tipping point? What would push it over? What emergent behavior am I seeing that can't be explained by any single component? What small change could trigger a phase transition?"

**FACE 6 -- CONSTRAINTS & BOTTLENECKS (D) -- Eliyahu Goldratt**
Every system has exactly one constraint that limits its throughput at any given time. Improving anything OTHER than the constraint is wasted effort. Identify the constraint, exploit it (maximize its capacity), subordinate everything else to it, elevate it (add capacity), and repeat. The constraint moves -- so must your attention.
*"An hour lost at the bottleneck is an hour lost for the entire system."*
Self-prompt: "What is the ONE constraint limiting this system's throughput right now? Am I improving the constraint, or wasting effort on non-constraints? If I removed this bottleneck, where would the new constraint appear?"

### OPPOSITE FACE PAIRS (Rotation Axes)

- **X-Axis (F<>B):** Feedback Loops x Leverage Points -- **How Systems Flow vs Where to Intervene**. Meadows' feedback loop analysis maps the dynamics -- what reinforces, what resists, what dominates. Her leverage points framework asks where to push for maximum effect. One is about understanding the system's behavior; the other is about choosing where to act. The tension reveals: "Do I understand the system well enough to know where to push, and am I pushing at a high-leverage point or a low one?"

- **Y-Axis (U<>D):** Emergence x Constraints -- **When Systems Tip vs What Holds Systems Back**. Kauffman and Gladwell look for tipping points where small changes create massive shifts -- phase transitions, viral cascades, emergent properties. Goldratt looks for the single bottleneck limiting throughput -- the constraint that determines the system's ceiling. One is about what accelerates; the other is about what limits. The tension reveals: "Is the system being held back by a constraint, or is it near a tipping point where removing the constraint would trigger a phase transition?"

- **Z-Axis (L<>R):** System Archetypes x Causal Loop Mapping -- **Pattern Recognition vs Causal Precision**. Senge identifies recurring system patterns at high level -- Limits to Growth, Shifting the Burden, Tragedy of the Commons. Sterman traces exact cause-and-effect chains with delays and polarities. One sees the forest; the other draws each tree. The tension reveals: "Have I correctly identified the pattern, and does the detailed causal map confirm or contradict my archetype diagnosis?"

## EXECUTION NOTES

1. **Concreteness over abstraction.** This stack is more abstract than others -- fight that tendency relentlessly. Name the specific loop. Draw the specific constraint. Don't say "feedback effects" -- say "customer complaints reduce team morale which reduces quality which increases complaints." Don't say "there are leverage points" -- say "the hiring policy (a rule-level leverage point) determines team size which determines response time which determines complaints."
2. **Honor the frameworks.** Each face represents a real, researched framework by a specific thinker. Apply it as its creator intended -- Meadows' 12 leverage points have a specific ranking, Goldratt's five focusing steps have a specific sequence, Senge's archetypes have specific structures. Then let the cross-analysis find the synthesis.
3. **The system is the Core.** Never lose sight of the specific system, its specific components, and its specific behaviors. Every rotation should move closer to a specific, executable intervention -- not a vague "improve the system."
4. **Draw the loops.** Whenever possible, use text-based causal loop notation to make structure visible: A --(+)--> B --(+)--> C --(-)--> A. Label delays. Mark reinforcing (R) and balancing (B) loops. The map IS the insight.
